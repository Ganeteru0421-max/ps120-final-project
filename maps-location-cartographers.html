Written responses

Continent and general location

Japan is an East Asian island arc east of Korea and Russia, south of Siberia, and northwest of the open Pacific (Central Intelligence Agency, n.d.). It sits on the western edge of the Pacific “Ring of Fire,” so plate boundaries offshore explain both the country’s steep terrain and its frequent earthquakes and volcanoes.

Latitudinal range

The World Factbook gives about 24°N to 46°N (Central Intelligence Agency, n.d.), matching the north–south stretch from Okinawa toward Hokkaidō. That span crosses several climate zones, from mild winters in the south to snow-heavy winters in the north.

When has Japan existed as a polity?

A long imperial line and early states reach back to antiquity; large samurai-led governments ran the country until the Tokugawa shogunate (1603–1868), then the Meiji state (from 1868) built today’s centralized nation and the current 1947 constitution ended the empire after 1945 (Gordon, 2020). Thinking about “Japan” therefore depends on whether we mean the imperial institution, the warrior-led government of a given century, or the postwar state defined by law after Allied occupation.

Cartographers

Inō Tadataka (1745–1818) led land and coastal surveys that produced Japan’s first widely trusted modern-style maps before the Meiji government published atlases (Encyclopædia Britannica, n.d.-a). Field parties combined distance chains along roads with astronomical sightings, which slowly corrected older sketches inherited from China or Europe.

Foreign knowledge still mattered. Matteo Ricci’s 1602 Chinese-language world map reached educated circles in East Asia and shaped debates about place names, scale, and world religion there, even though Ricci himself worked mainly in China (Encyclopædia Britannica, n.d.-b). Together, imported worldviews and domestic survey illustrate two sides of Japan’s cartographic history.
